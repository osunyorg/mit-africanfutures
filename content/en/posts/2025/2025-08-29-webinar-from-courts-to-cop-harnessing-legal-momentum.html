On August 25, African Futures Lab, in partnership with AU Economic, Social and Cultural Council (ECOSOCC) and Biophilic Conversations , hosted the webinar "From Courts to COP: Harnessing Legal Momentum

for African Climate Reparations." Moderated by our Research and Policy Advisor, Dr. Patrick Toussaint , the event brought together leading experts to discuss how recent landmark rulings are empowering Africa's position in global climate negotiations.
The panel explored recent advisory opinions from the International Court of Justice, the Inter-American Court of Human Rights, and the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea. Speakers highlighted how these rulings create binding legal obligations for states to prevent climate harm and deliver reparations.
Legal experts affirmed that these breakthroughs give African advocates new leverage. The discussion also highlighted the potential of a pending advisory opinion from the African Court on Human and Peoples’ Rights to further strengthen the continent’s bargaining power ahead of COP30. Speakers noted that advisory opinions provide legal backing for the climate struggle and open the door to holding corporate polluters accountable.
This webinar forms part of a broader wave of momentum – from growing grassroots mobilization to coordinated legal strategies – building towards African Climate Week, the Second African Climate Summit, and COP30 in Belém. Courts have spoken. Now it’s Africa’s turn to translate these rulings into action, shifting the balance of power and shaping a future rooted in justice and accountability.

Key Messages from the Webinar


The climate advisory opinions affirm states’ legal duties:  The recent advisory opinions from the ICJ, IACtHR and ITLOS confirm that states have clear obligations to protect human rights, prevent climate harm, and provide remedies. This gives African actors stronger grounds to demand accountability.

The petition for an advisory opinion from the AfCHPR centers on African realities and law:   The request aims to ground climate reparations in Africa’s own human rights framework, especially Article 24 of the African Charter on the right to a healthy environment. It can strengthen Africa’s legal basis to demand accountability, complement the ICJ, IACtHR & ITLOS opinions, and provide a regionally rooted framework for strategic litigation and advocacy.

Local knowledge strengthens climate justice: Recognizing Indigenous knowledge, customary law, and community expertise ensures solutions are relevant and builds ownership of climate justice efforts across Africa.


Science and lived realities must guide strategic litigation:  The courts recognized the role of science and the disproportionate impact on vulnerable communities. Strategic legal action for climate reparations benefits from robust documentation of the lived realities, and advances in attribution science to link climate harms to state and corporate responsibility.


Reparations address past and present harm while laying the foundation for future claims and long-term justice. Women and other marginalised groups must be central to such demands.


Reparations can be transformative and go beyond financial compensation: Meaningful reparations tackle systemic inequalities and historical injustices, delivering lasting change rather than short-term payouts. They include ecological restoration, social justice & rehabilitation of essential systems, ecosystem restoration, debt cancellation, acknowledgment of past and ongoing injustices, and guarantees of non-repetition.


Climate finance should not be framed as aid:  Financial contributions to repair climate damage should be based on historical responsibility and reparative justice, not charity.


Past and present injustices must be fully acknowledged and addressed: Reparations must account for centuries of slavery, colonialism, extractive exploitation, and ongoing illicit financial flows, ensuring that stolen wealth and resources are restored to Africa.


Reparations strategies should be locally led to be context-specific: Grassroots voices, Indigenous knowledge, and frontline defenders must be re-powered, ensuring that reparations agendas reflect community needs and lived realities. Many African communities already understand the concept of reparations, even if they use different terms; advocacy should build on this local understanding to drive action.


Climate reparations serve to uphold people’s dignity and protect ecosystems: The narrative should affirm the worth of marginalized communities and degraded environments, linking human and ecological well-being.


The time is ripe for Africa to build solidarity and shared narratives:  Lessons from Caribbean and Latin American movements can strengthen Africa’s reparations demands. South-South alliances and accessible language make it easier for communities to understand, claim, and shape reparations.

Meet the Speakers
Patrick is an international environmental lawyer based in East Africa, specializing in climate justice and human rights.  He works at the intersection of climate change, biodiversity, clean air, plastic pollution, and sustainable development, with a focus on the legal and policy dimensions of loss and damage. Patrick holds a Doctor of Laws in international climate law, with research on human rights-based approaches, legal responsibility, and pathways for advancing climate justice.
Dr. Patrick Toussaint
Research and Policy Advisor, African Futures Lab

With over two decades of international development experience, William Carew has held senior leadership roles at organizations including the African Development Bank, ActionAid, and World Vision.  An expert in strengthening civil society, his work within the African Union framework focuses on empowering communities to champion climate justice and build grassroots movements for reparations. Mr. Carew is a dedicated advocate for inclusive development and a Ph.D. candidate in Development Planning and Public Administration at the University of Stellenbosch, South Africa.
William Carew
Head of AU ECOSOCC Secretariat

Mr. Kyeretwie Osei directs programmess at the African Union’s ECOSOCC, where he manages technical resources and leads partnership development.  He brings extensive expertise in civil society and diaspora policy, honed during his four years as a Policy Officer at the African Union Commission.  Prior to that, Mr. Osei spent seven years leading the African Diaspora Program for Cuso International including several years leading their African Diaspora Program.
Kyeretwie Osei
Head of Programmes, AU ECOSOCC Secretariat

Mary Muthoni is an attorney with over 8 years of experience in environmental law, climate justice, and biodiversity conservation.  She consults for the Future Generations Tribunal, advocating for intergenerational rights, and serves on the board of the Tony Wild Foundation.  Mary is an expert in legal research and policy reform, with contributions to global frameworks like the UNFCCC COP28 International Youth Climate Delegate, co-developing the People’s Petition for the ICJ Advisory Opinion on Climate Change, and participating in the IUCN Africa Congress. 
Mary Muthoni
Co-director, Biophilic Conversations

Lucien Limacher is a qualified attorney and mediator with over a decade of experience in environmental law.  Lucien holds an LLB and LLM from the University of the Witwatersrand and is a qualified attorney and mediator.  At Natural Justice, he leads strategic workstreams across Africa, ensuring program innovation and impact. Lucien has contributed to over 30 strategic litigation cases, bringing a unique perspective to legal strategy. 
Lucien Limacher
Head of Defending Rights and Litigation, Natural Justice

Henrietta Ekefre is an international lawyer specializing in reparations and transformative justice. With over a decade of experience, she has advised human rights bodies and led the Africa Reparations Program.  Ms. Henrietta is an inaugural fellow at the Clooney Foundation for Justice, and has advanced legal strategies before African Union and UN mechanisms. Her expertise spans women’s rights, transitional justice, and accountability processes. She holds degrees in law, development, and human rights from the University of The Gambia (LLB), the University of Pretoria (LLM), Loyola University Chicago (LLM), and the University of Oxford (MSc).
Henrietta Ekefre
Transformative Justice, Gender and Reparations Expert

Junecynthia Okelo is a qualified advocate of the High Court of Kenya. She leverages her skills and expertise in climate justice and economic rights to provide economic governance support to the pan-African legal profession.  With her expertise and continental legal frameworks, Junecynthia brings crucial African perspectives to our discussion of the African Court on Human and Peoples' Rights' advisory opinion request and how it boosts in harnessing the legal momentum for African climate reparations.
Junecynthia Okelo
Economic Governance Officer and Climate Justice Lead, PALU

Samira Ben Ali is a lawyer and climate justice advocate from the island of Mayotte.  Samira works as the Global Campaigns Lead for the World’s Youth for Climate Justice where she played a major role in the youth campaign that brought climate justice before the ICJ.  Samira also has a passion for ocean conservation, that she put into action as the Ocean Project Assistant for Youth and Environment Europe.  Additionally, she serves as the EESC Youth Delegate to COP (2025 & 2026) and is the IUCN World Commission on Environmental Law Early Career Specialist Group representative to the IUCN Youth Advisory Committee for 2025. 
Samira Ben Ali
Global Campaigns Lead for the World’s Youth for Climate Justice
